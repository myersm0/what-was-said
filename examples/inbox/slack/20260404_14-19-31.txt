Is stretch tuning really necessary on small uprights?

1 reply

Dana Willis  [2:13 PM]
Less critical than grands, but still helps avoid beating in upper octaves. The treble strings are shorter and stiffer, so inharmonicity is inherent.

R_Kim  [2:14 PM]
Do you calculate stretch or do it by ear?

Dana Willis  [2:16 PM]
Mostly by ear, but some use apps for a starting curve. I've tried both—the apps are good references if you're learning.

M. Liu  [3:05 PM]
The key thing with uprights is that they have less mass in the soundboard, so stretch becomes audible earlier in the frequency range than on a grand. Even a small upright benefits from it.

R_Kim  [3:07 PM]
How much stretch are we talking? Like a few cents across the whole keyboard?

Dana Willis  [3:09 PM]
For a small upright, maybe 5–10 cents total across the full range. Grands can go 15–20 cents depending on the action and soundboard mass.

M. Liu  [3:12 PM]
Also depends on the age. Older uprights with thinner soundboards need more stretch. Newer ones with reinforced designs can get away with less.

R_Kim  [3:14 PM]
Okay, so I should experiment on my particular instrument rather than follow a fixed formula.

Dana Willis  [3:16 PM]
Exactly right. And if you make a mistake, you can always retune. The beauty of stretch tuning is it's not permanent—just a tuning choice.
