What's the best way to prevent tear-out when cross-cutting plywood?

3 replies

sam_ortega
Yesterday at 6:03 PM
Use a zero-clearance insert and a sharp blade with high tooth count.

jon_m
Yesterday at 6:04 PM
Would blue tape help as well?

sam_ortega
Yesterday at 6:05 PM
Yes, tape over the cut line reduces splintering. The tape supports the veneer fibers on both sides of the kerf.

jon_m
Yesterday at 6:06 PM
Great, thanks.

sam_ortega
Yesterday at 6:09 PM
You can also score the cut line lightly before sawing. A sharp chisel or scoring knife works well.

mford
Yesterday at 6:15 PM
Another trick: use the table saw with the blade set higher than usual—just barely through the material. Keeps the blade cooler and reduces friction chatter.

sam_ortega
Yesterday at 6:17 PM
That's counterintuitive but yeah, lower angle of blade through the kerf helps reduce splinter. Though you need really sharp teeth or you'll get more tear-out.

mford
Yesterday at 6:20 PM
Exactly. I usually combine all three: zero-clearance insert, high tooth count blade, and tape. Haven't had tear-out on 3/4" birch plywood in years.

Is a track saw better for this than a table saw?

mford
Yesterday at 6:25 PM
Track saws are excellent for long cross-cuts and they have natural downforce from gravity, so less tear-out. But the blade speed is usually lower than a table saw, so you need to be even more careful with tooth geometry.

Elena V
Yesterday at 6:30 PM
I prefer hand tools for veneer plywood. A fine-toothed handsaw and a sharp chisel for cleanup takes longer but zero tear-out, every time.

sam_ortega
Yesterday at 6:32 PM
That works too, but most people need the throughput of a power tool. If you're batch-cutting sheet goods, the table saw route with all the precautions is fastest.
