eli_c  [7:41 AM]
Any tips for keeping joints square when gluing cabinets?

1 reply

rosa_d  [7:45 AM]
Use clamps diagonally and check with a framing square. The diagonal should pull the frame back into square if you apply pressure perpendicular to the long diagonal.

eli_c  [7:47 AM]
Do corner clamps help?

rosa_d  [7:49 AM]
Yes, especially for small boxes. They apply even pressure across the joint and reduce the number of clamps you need.

eli_c  [7:52 AM]
Perfect, thanks!

rosa_d  [7:55 AM]
One more thing: dry-fit the whole assembly first. Find out which clamps you need and in what order before you apply glue.

mford  [8:05 AM]
That's the golden rule. Take a photo of the dry-fit setup too. Helps you remember the sequence once glue is involved. (edited)

rosa_d  [8:08 AM]
Absolutely. And label your clamps if you have a lot of them. Nothing worse than grabbing the wrong size mid-glue-up.

eli_c  [8:10 AM]
Good points. What about glue squeeze-out? Does that affect joint strength?

mford  [8:13 AM]
Slight squeeze-out is good—it means you have enough glue. Wipe it off before it sets with a damp cloth, but don't obsess over perfect cleanup during the glue-up. You can chisel off dried squeeze-out later.

rosa_d  [8:16 AM]
If you're using hide glue, clean-up is easier because it stays liquid longer. For PVA, you have a smaller window.

Elena V  [8:20 AM]
I've had good luck with epoxy for cabinet work because the glue line is more water-resistant and it doesn't require perfect clamping pressure like PVA does.

mford  [8:23 AM]
Epoxy's slower to set, which gives you more time, but it's messier to clean up. And you pay a premium for it.
